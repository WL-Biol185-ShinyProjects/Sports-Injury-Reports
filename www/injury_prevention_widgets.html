Wear the right shoes key factor

Sport-specific footwear matters

▼
  • Choose shoes designed for your specific sport — running shoes for forward motion, cross-trainers for lateral movement
  • Look for good arch support, a stable heel base, and cushioning under the ball of the foot
  • Replace shoes regularly — worn-out soles lose the support that protects your ankles
  • Try shoes on at the end of the day when feet are slightly swollen for the most accurate fit
🔄

Warm up before activity essential

5–10 min before every session

▼
  • Always warm up for at least 5–10 minutes before any physical activity
  • Include ankle circles, leg swings, and light jogging to increase blood flow
  • Use dynamic stretches that mimic the movements of your sport
  • Start at low intensity and gradually build up to your full workout pace
💪

Strengthen your ankle muscles essential

Calf raises & resistance bands

▼
  • Do heel raises daily — rise onto the balls of your feet and slowly lower back down, 10 reps
  • Use a resistance band for ankle eversion — this trains the peroneal muscles that act as a natural brace
  • Gradually condition your leg and ankle muscles — don't rush into high-intensity activity
  • Repeat exercises 10–15 times, 3 sets per day
⚖️

Train your balance & proprioception

Your ankle's "sixth sense"

▼
  • Balance on one foot for 30 seconds — progress to eyes closed or unstable surfaces
  • Proprioception is critical — strong ankles can still roll if your brain can't react in time
  • Use a balance board or wobble cushion for more advanced training
  • These exercises help your ankle react before a roll becomes a sprain
🧘

Stretch your calves regularly

Especially the lower calf (soleus)

▼
  • Soleus stretch: step one foot back, bend both knees, keep heels flat — hold 15–30 seconds
  • Also stretch the gastrocnemius: back leg straight, lean into a wall, heel on floor
  • Best done after exercise when muscles are warm — make it part of your cool-down
  • Stretch both legs equally; consistency beats intensity
🩹

Brace or tape if you've had a previous sprain if applicable

External support for high-risk activities

▼
  • A previous sprain leaves ligaments lax and increases re-injury risk — bracing helps during recovery
  • Use a snug-but-not-tight brace; it should not restrict blood flow
  • Gradually wean off the brace as strength and confidence return
  • Bracing is a supplement — not a replacement — for strengthening and balance work
